Ladies’ Fine Gloves (circa 1830 to 1850)

In front of you is a pair of ladies’ leather gloves, made around 1830 to 1850. The leather is thin and supple, and the seams along the fingers are stitched by hand. They narrow gently at the wrist and were made to be worn close to the skin.

They are made from fine leather, often lamb or kid skin, chosen because it could be cut thin without tearing and would stretch slightly with wear.

Each glove was cut by hand and stitched finger by finger with fine thread. The work required good light, steady hands, and patience.

Gloves like these were worn for walking, visiting, church, and travel. They were practical, but also carried a quiet sense of care and refinement.

These gloves show the quiet skill behind everyday life — not only the hands that wore them, but the hands that made them.

Steel Sewing Needle (circa 1830 to 1850)

In front of you is a steel sewing needle, made around 1830 to 1850. It is small, sharp, and easy to miss, with a tiny eye for thread. It fits easily between finger and thumb and would have been used thousands of times.

It is made from hardened steel, polished so it could pass smoothly through leather.

It was threaded and pulled through leather again and again to sew gloves by hand. The work was repetitive and precise.

It was most often used by women working at home, fitting paid sewing around daily responsibilities.

This needle represents invisible labour — the process between raw material and finished artefact.
